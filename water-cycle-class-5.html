Water Cycle Diagram for Class 5: Complete Study Guide

Understanding the Water Cycle for Class 5

The water cycle, also called the hydrological cycle, is the continuous movement of water on, above, and below the surface of the Earth. This process has been happening for billions of years and is essential for all life on our planet.

Why is the Water Cycle Important?

The water cycle ensures that water is constantly recycled and purified naturally. It provides fresh water for drinking, supports plant growth, regulates Earth's temperature, and shapes our weather patterns.

5 Stages of the Water Cycle Explained

1Evaporation - How Water Vapor Forms

Definition: Evaporation is the process by which liquid water changes into water vapor (gas) and rises into the atmosphere.

How it works:

  • The sun heats water in oceans, lakes, rivers, and soil
  • Heat energy breaks the bonds between water molecules
  • Water molecules escape into the air as invisible water vapor
  • Higher temperatures cause faster evaporation

Key Points:

  • About 90% of water vapor comes from oceans
  • Water doesn't disappear - it changes state from liquid to gas
  • This process requires heat energy from the sun (solar energy)

2Transpiration - Water from Plants

Definition: Transpiration is the process where plants release water vapor through tiny openings in their leaves called stomata.

How it works:

  • Plants absorb water from soil through their roots
  • Water travels up through the plant's stem
  • Water evaporates from leaves into the atmosphere
  • This helps plants cool down and transport nutrients

Important: Evaporation from water bodies + Transpiration from plants = Evapotranspiration

3Condensation - Clouds Form

Definition: Condensation is the process where water vapor (gas) cools down and changes back into liquid water droplets.

How it works:

  • Water vapor rises high into the atmosphere
  • As it goes higher, the air temperature decreases
  • Cool air cannot hold as much water vapor
  • Water vapor condenses around dust particles or salt crystals
  • Millions of tiny water droplets join together to form clouds

Examples of condensation:

  • Dew drops forming on grass in the morning
  • Fog near the ground
  • Water droplets on cold glass
  • Clouds in the sky

4Precipitation - Water Falls

Definition: Precipitation is any form of water that falls from clouds to the Earth's surface.

Types of Precipitation:

  • Rain: Liquid water drops (most common)
  • Snow: Ice crystals (when temperature is below freezing)
  • Sleet: Frozen raindrops
  • Hail: Ice pellets formed during thunderstorms

How it forms:

  • Water droplets in clouds bump into each other and merge
  • Droplets grow bigger and heavier
  • When droplets become too heavy, they fall as precipitation
  • The type depends on air temperature

5Collection/Infiltration - Water Returns

Definition: Collection is when water that falls as precipitation gathers in rivers, lakes, and oceans.

What happens to precipitation:

  • Surface Runoff: Water flows over land into streams and rivers
  • Infiltration: Water soaks into the ground
  • Groundwater: Water stored underground in soil and rocks
  • Returns to oceans: Rivers carry water back to the sea

The cycle repeats: Once water reaches water bodies, the sun heats it again, and evaporation starts the cycle all over!

Factors Affecting the Water Cycle

Temperature

Higher temperature increases evaporation rate. Warmer air can hold more water vapor than cold air.

Sunlight

More sunlight provides more energy for evaporation. This is why evaporation is faster during summer than winter.

Humidity

Humidity is the amount of water vapor in the air. High humidity slows down evaporation because the air is already holding a lot of water vapor.

Wind and Air Pressure

Wind carries water vapor from one place to another. Air pressure differences cause clouds to move and affect where precipitation occurs.

Common Water Cycle Questions for Class 5 Exams

Short Answer Questions

Q1: What is evaporation?
Answer: Evaporation is the process by which liquid water is converted into water vapor (gas) due to heat from the sun. It occurs mainly from oceans, lakes, rivers, and soil.
Q2: Define condensation.
Answer: Condensation is the process where water vapor in the atmosphere cools down and changes back into liquid water droplets, forming clouds.
Q3: Name the different forms of precipitation.
Answer: The different forms of precipitation are:
  • Rain (liquid water)
  • Snow (ice crystals)
  • Sleet (frozen rain)
  • Hail (ice pellets)
Q4: What is transpiration?
Answer: Transpiration is the process by which plants release water vapor into the atmosphere through small openings in their leaves called stomata.
Q5: Why is the water cycle important?
Answer: The water cycle is important because it:
  • Provides fresh water for drinking and agriculture
  • Regulates Earth's temperature and climate
  • Purifies water naturally
  • Supports all plant and animal life
  • Creates weather patterns

Long Answer Questions

Q1: Explain the complete water cycle with all 5 stages.
Answer: The water cycle consists of 5 main stages:
  1. Evaporation: The sun heats water in oceans, lakes, and rivers, converting it into water vapor that rises into the atmosphere.
  2. Transpiration: Plants release water vapor through their leaves into the atmosphere.
  3. Condensation: Water vapor rises, cools down at higher altitudes, and changes back into tiny water droplets, forming clouds.
  4. Precipitation: When cloud droplets become heavy, they fall to Earth as rain, snow, sleet, or hail.
  5. Collection: Water collects in rivers, lakes, and oceans, or soaks into the ground. The cycle then repeats.
Q2: How does the sun play a role in the water cycle?
Answer: The sun is the driving force of the water cycle. It provides heat energy (solar energy) that:
  • Causes water to evaporate from water bodies
  • Warms the air, allowing it to hold water vapor
  • Drives the entire cycle by providing energy
Without the sun's heat, there would be no evaporation, and the water cycle would not function.
Q3: What happens to water after it falls as rain?
Answer: After falling as rain, water follows different paths:
  • Surface Runoff: Flows over land into streams, rivers, and eventually oceans
  • Infiltration: Soaks into the soil and becomes groundwater
  • Collection: Gathers in lakes, ponds, and reservoirs
  • Plant Absorption: Absorbed by plant roots for growth
  • Evaporation: Some water evaporates directly from surfaces
Eventually, all this water returns to the water cycle through evaporation or transpiration.

Study Tips for Mastering the Water Cycle

Learning Strategies

  • Draw and label the water cycle diagram 3-4 times from memory
  • Use the acronym "E-T-C-P-C" for the 5 stages: Evaporation, Transpiration, Condensation, Precipitation, Collection
  • Create flashcards for vocabulary terms
  • Explain each stage to a friend or family member

Practice Methods

  • Cover the answers and try answering the questions above
  • Draw arrows showing the direction of water movement
  • Use different colors for different stages
  • Observe real examples: clouds, rain, puddles evaporating

Exam Preparation

  • Know all 5 stages and their definitions
  • Understand the role of the sun in the water cycle
  • Be able to draw and label a complete water cycle diagram
  • Practice short and long answer questions
  • Remember real-world examples for each stage
